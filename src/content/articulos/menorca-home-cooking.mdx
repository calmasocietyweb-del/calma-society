The best cooking in Menorca hardly ever appears on a menu. It lives in the homes, follows the year's calendar —the pig slaughter, Easter, the tomato of summer— and passes from one generation to the next with no written recipe. It is a cooking of **spoon and oven**, of thrift and of feast, that explains the island better than any restaurant.

## The essentials

- ***Oliaigua*** —"oli" and "aigua", oil and water— is Menorca's mother soup: humble, of summer, and eaten **cold with figs or melon**.
- ***Arròs de la terra*** deceives: it has **no rice**, but **crushed *xeixa* wheat**, oven-baked with cured meats. It is a dish of the *matances*.
- The ***greixera*** is not a dish but a **family** of oven stews baked in an earthenware pot; there are savoury ones and sweet ones (the one made with *brossat*, curd cheese).
- **Easter** has its own baking: ***formatjades*, *rubiols*, *cocarrois*, *flaons* and *crespells***.
- Almost all are dishes **of season and of feast**: knowing when they are eaten is half the recipe.

## The spoon: oliaigua

If Menorca has a mother soup, it is the *oliaigua*. The name says it all: *oli* and *aigua*, oil and water, and from there onion, tomato, pepper, garlic and stale bread. It was born as country food —whatever was in the pantry— and today it is a summer classic served **cold, with figs or melon**, in that sweet-savoury play so Mediterranean. **Pere Ballester** already documented it in *De re cibaria*, in **1923**, proof that it is cooking with deep roots.

*(One note, because it misleads: the *sopes* with a name of their own are Mallorcan. In Menorca, the soup of the house is the oliaigua.)*

## The oven: arròs de la terra and greixeres

The most misunderstood dish on the island is the *arròs de la terra*: it has **no rice**. It is made with *blat xeixa* —a local wheat— crushed in the stone mortar, soaked and **oven-baked with potato, garlic and cured meats**. It is the dish of the *matances*, the pig slaughter, and its origin points to Andalusi cooking. The Consell de Menorca's own agri-food network lists it as heritage.

The other great oven family are the *greixeres*, which take their name from the earthenware pot in which they are baked. There are **savoury** *greixeres* —of meat, of cheese, of potato, even of *arròs de la terra*— and **sweet** ones, led by the *greixera de brossat*, a pudding of curd cheese, egg and sugar with the texture of a custard, documented on the island since old times.

## The festive calendar

Much of this cooking is tied to the calendar:

- **Easter:** the *formatjades* (savoury pies of meat —lamb or pork— and *sobrassada*) and the *rubiols* and *cocarrois* (half-moon turnovers, sweet or vegetable), plus the *flaons* and *crespells*. Some, such as the *rubiols*, point to a **Sephardic Jewish heritage** later "Christianised".
- **Winter / matances:** the *arròs de la terra*, the fresh cured meats and the meat *greixeres*.
- **Summer:** the cold *oliaigua*, at the height of the tomato and fig season; and the *perol*, layers of potato and tomato baked in the oven, sometimes with fish in between.
- **Sant Joan (23 June) and festivals:** festive sweets such as the *coca bamba*.

## Where and when to try them

These dishes do not live on menus but on the calendar. The rule is simple: **go when the moment comes**. At **Easter**, the *formatjades* and *rubiols* fill the **bakeries** across the island. In **winter**, the *arròs de la terra* and the cured meats appear at the **produce fairs**. And in **summer**, the *oliaigua* is a dish of the tomato season. The **weekly markets** of Maó, Ciutadella, Es Mercadal or Ferreries and the agri-food *fires* are the best places for the sweets and cured meats —though do confirm dates and venues each season, because they change.

## Our view

To eat the real Menorca is a matter of **timing, not of booking**: coinciding with the Easter of the *formatjades*, the summer of the *oliaigua*, the fair where someone is selling their *arròs de la terra*. No star gives what a home-made *greixera* gives. Our advice: when you see one of these dishes in its season, do not let it pass.

> Fine dining is booked; home cooking is found. In Menorca, the second is the one that truly tells the year.
