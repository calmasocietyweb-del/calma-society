"Is Menorca expensive to live in?" is one of those questions with a misleading answer. If you want a round number — *so many euros a month and done* — you'll find it on any comparison site, stripped of context and out of date before you've finished reading it. The truth is more interesting and more useful: living here is neither as dear as some fear nor as cheap as others promise, but it **costs in a different way** from any life on the mainland. And that difference — the insularity, the seasonality, the car — is exactly what no one ever puts in a table.

This is not a spreadsheet. The specific cost figures change every season, anyone can summarise them, and above all they **don't tell you how you actually live on that money on a Tuesday in February**. What really decides whether Menorca adds up for you is something else: how you get around, which months you come, what you live on, and how much you value what the island gives back in return. ## Is Menorca expensive to live in? The honest answer

Let's begin with what can be said with some confidence: **the cost of living in Menorca is somewhat above the Spanish average.** Not outrageously so, but perceptibly. And the main reason has a name: **insularity**.

Everything not produced on the island arrives by sea (or, when it's urgent, by air), and that journey is paid for. You feel it in building materials, in deliveries, in repairs, in certain supermarket products, and in the cost of bringing over a specialist who is in short supply locally. It isn't that everything is dearer — local produce, fish, seasonal vegetables can be very good value — but rather that **what depends on the outside carries a structural mark-up** that simply doesn't exist on the mainland.

It's wise to treat any global "monthly cost of living" figure with caution: it depends so much on how you live — village or coast, rented or owned, one car or two, how often you eat out — that a single number misleads more than it informs. Take the references in this article as an **indicative order of magnitude as of June 2026**, not a closed budget, and check whatever truly matters to you with people who live here and with the official sources. In this, honesty is worth more than false precision.

## What no one tells you about the real cost

Here is the part the comparisons skip and which, even so, is the one that truly determines your finances on the island. They are three costs that appear in no table because they aren't a figure: they are a way of living.

**Insularity charges you in the details.** It isn't only that a new fridge arrives dearer; it's that the technician who installs it may take longer to come, that the spare part travels over from the mainland, and that renovating or maintaining a house calls for a different calendar and budget from the continent's. Anyone who has a house here — and all the more so a [stone house that asks for craft and care](/en/article/stone-house) — soon learns that insularity isn't a surcharge on the bill but a way of planning life.

**February costs in a different way.** In summer everything is open, everything works, everything is immediate; in winter, not so. Much of the coastal dining and retail closes off-season, and sorting certain things out — finding a plumber, buying something specific, a specialist errand — asks for patience or a trip. That cost isn't measured in euros but in friction, and it is exactly the friction that keeps the island as it is. On what that winter is really like — and why it pays off for some — we write at length in [living in Menorca year-round](/en/article/living-in-menorca-year-round).

**Summer inflates the mirage.** The Menorca of August — full terraces, high prices, everything at capacity — is not the Menorca you'll live in for eleven months. If you work out your cost of living using high-season prices, you'll frighten yourself unnecessarily; if you reckon it on February alone, you'll fall short when July comes. Real life sits in the honest average of the twelve months, and that average is kinder than it looks from the summer postcard.

## The resident discount: the great difference

If there is one factor that separates the cost of **visiting** Menorca from that of **living** it, it's the resident discount. Anyone registered as a resident on the island is entitled to a reduction on air and ferry tickets to the rest of the country, which markedly lowers the cost of moving off the island — the great recurring expense of any islander.

It is probably the best piece of financial news about moving here: travelling to the mainland, which is dear for the visitor, becomes far more manageable for the resident. It changes the equation of "living on an island" entirely, and it is the reason why, for many people, insularity stops weighing the moment they register.

That said, **prudence calls for an important caveat:** the exact conditions, requirements and percentages of this discount depend on current legislation and have been changed in the past. Before making firm calculations that count on a specific percentage, **confirm it at the official source** (the information from the Government and from the carriers themselves). The principle — the resident pays considerably less to get around — is solid; the exact figure is best verified on the day you need it.

## The car: the fixed line almost everyone forgets

Before talking about food or leisure, an expense that weighs more than people think: **to live in Menorca all year round, a car is all but essential.** It isn't a recommendation for comfort, it's a structural reality. Public transport exists, but it is limited and very seasonal; the distances between villages, coves, the main hospital or the airport are covered by car.

That means a fixed line worth putting in the budget from day one: the vehicle, its insurance, fuel, maintenance and — the further your house is from a town, the more kilometres each week without your noticing. A beautiful, isolated house in the countryside is wonderful until you count it in daily journeys — the shopping, the doctor, your social life. There's no need to give it up, but do budget for it with your eyes open: living far from a town is also a cost decision.

## Eating, going out and everyday life: the daily cost

In day-to-day spending, Menorca offers some pleasant surprises, especially off-season. The **set lunch menu** (*menú del día*) holds within a reasonable band — around **€12–15** as a reference **indicative as of June 2026** — and in winter the prices of leisure, dining and services drop below the summer peak. The island you live for eleven months is considerably cheaper than the one you visit in August.

Local produce helps to balance the basket: **Mahón-Menorca PDO** cheese, fresh fish, seasonal vegetables and the weekly market let you eat very well without insularity biting too hard. Where it's worth keeping an eye is on the imported and the specialist, which is where the island mark-up shows. As throughout this article, treat these figures as a snapshot of a moment: they guide, they don't close a budget, and they expire. The sensible thing is to check them on the ground before you move.

## What do people live on here? The cost has an income side

To talk about how much it costs to live in Menorca without talking about how people earn a living would be to tell half the story. And here the fact is honest and far from glamorous: **work on the island is very seasonal.** Much of the activity revolves around tourism, which concentrates the jobs in the warm months and leaves winters of low activity. Living on a local wage all year round is possible, but not always easy or well paid.

That's why the profile that fits best all 365 days tends to be someone who **doesn't depend on the island's labour market**: remote work (income from outside, costs from here), active retirement, and those who live on private income. It isn't an absolute rule — there are people who earn a very good living on the island all year — but it is the pattern most often repeated among those who arrive from elsewhere and stay. If your plan is to work remotely, digital connectivity has improved a great deal and makes it viable in most towns; do, however, verify the specific coverage in your area before committing.

The financial reading is clear: in Menorca, the cost of living is far easier to carry when your income doesn't depend on its seasonal economy. Those who bring their work or their income with them find one of the best ratios of quality of life to spending in the Mediterranean; those who expect to live solely on what the island offers in winter will have to budget more finely.

## So, does it add up?

It depends on what you put in the "credit" column. If all you place on the scales is euros, Menorca comes out somewhat dearer than the Spanish average, and that's that. But the honest reckoning includes what you receive in return: safety, protected nature on your doorstep, a rhythm that gives you back your time, a real community, produce and sea. None of that appears in any cost-of-living table, and it is precisely what makes the sum come out positive for so many people.

The sincere conclusion is this: **Menorca is not the cheapest island in the Mediterranean, but neither is it the dear trap some fear.** It is a moderate-to-high cost of living that two things make much cheaper — the resident discount and truly living all twelve months, not just the summer — and that two others make dearer — insularity on the imported and dependence on the car. Anyone who understands those four variables budgets well and almost never gets a nasty shock.

## Our take

The most common mistake when working out the cost of living in Menorca is to look for a number and believe it. That number doesn't exist: what exists is your way of living applied to the rules of an island. We've seen people arrive with a mainland spreadsheet only to discover that the lines here are different ones — less in some, more in others they didn't expect — and others fixate on the price per square metre of a house, forgetting that the car, the winter and the plane tickets weigh as much as the mortgage.
