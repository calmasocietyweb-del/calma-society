Few places in the Mediterranean have their own gin. Menorca does, and the story is real: during the **British rule of the 18th century**, sailors wanted gin, and the island's distillers began making it with what they had at hand.

## A gin with a Menorcan accent

**Gin Xoriguer** is still distilled in **Maó** in copper stills, from wine alcohol and juniper berries. You'll know it by its handled bottles —the *canecas*— inspired by those sailors once used. This is no trend gin: it has centuries behind it.

## Pomada: the drink of the festes

The most Menorcan way to drink it is **pomada**: gin with cloudy lemonade, ice-cold. It's the drink of every town's *festes* —from Sant Joan to Gràcia— sipped among horses, music and crowds.

- **Always cold.** Warm pomada doesn't exist.
- **In moderation.** It flows easily at the festes; summer is long.
- **Better with real produce:** proper lemon, plenty of ice.

> To taste pomada isn't to taste a cocktail: it's to step, for a moment, into the festival that defines the island.
