Menorca arrives in 2026 with a paradox that describes its table better than any slogan: **it is the only Balearic island without a Michelin star** —Mallorca gathers twelve stars across eleven restaurants— and, at the same time, its cooking is enjoying the broadest recognition anyone can remember. Eleven restaurants recommended by the red guide, four hotels holding a Michelin Key, and a steady drip of Repsol Sols that adds a new house almost every year.

For those who understand luxury the way we do here —calm, produce and human scale— that paradox is not a flaw: it is a portrait. The island lacks the trophy (for now), but it has something far harder to manufacture: a network of small, deeply personal dining rooms where you eat memorably without choreography. This is the map of that fine dining, with names, dates and verified facts, so you can book with judgement.

## The essentials

- **No Michelin star in the 2026 guide.** Menorca is the only Balearic island without one; Mallorca holds twelve stars in eleven restaurants.
- **Eleven Menorcan restaurants "Recommended" by the 2026 Michelin Guide**, two more than in 2025. The newcomers: Candela and La Cocina de Cristine, both in Maó.
- **Four hotels with one Michelin Key** since November 2024: Torralbenc, Can Albertí 1740, Cristine Bedfor and Faustino Gran Relais & Châteaux.
- **The Repsol Sols keep growing:** veterans Smoix and Cafè Balear were joined by El Romero and Sa Llagosta (2024), Sa Pedrera d'es Pujol (2025) and Aquiara (2026).
- **Almost everything is seasonal.** Most of these tables open from spring to autumn and work with few covers: always book, and always confirm hours.

## Why doesn't Menorca have a Michelin star?

The question hovers over every edition of the guide, and the answer given by experts and the local press points to two causes that have little to do with the level of the cooking. The first is **seasonality**: an island that concentrates its activity into a few months struggles to sustain, year-round, the structure —team, service, regularity— that Michelin demands of a starred restaurant. The second: the **inspectors' limited itineraries**, which bring them to the island less often than to other destinations.

That absence is best read without drama. The star rewards one specific model of restaurant; its lack describes not what is eaten here, but how the island's economy works. In fact, the red guide has never stopped flagging Menorcan tables — it simply does so, for now, one rung below stardom.

## What does the Michelin Guide recognise on the island?

In its 2026 edition, the Michelin Guide lists **eleven Menorcan restaurants as "Recommended"** —two more than in 2025— whose plaques were presented at a ceremony at CaixaForum in Palma in March 2026:

- **Candela** (Maó), new for 2026, opposite the market, with the day's seafood chalked on a board.
- **[La Cocina de Cristine](/en/place/restaurante-la-cocina-de-cristine-en)** (Maó), the other newcomer: the restaurant of the Cristine Bedfor hotel, with Menorcan chef Pau Sintes.
- **Santa Mariana** (Alaior), the family agrotourism with its own vegetable garden and olive oil.
- **[El Romero](/en/place/restaurante-el-romero-en)** (Maó), the sustainable table on plaça de la Conquesta.
- **[Sa Pedrera d'es Pujol](/en/place/restaurante-sa-pedrera-des-pujol-en)** (Sant Lluís), the country house of Daniel Mora and Nuria Pendás.
- **[Smoix](/en/place/restaurante-smoix-en)** (Ciutadella), which also keeps the Bib Gourmand it has held since 2023.
- **Godai** (Cala en Bosc, Ciutadella), chef Julián Mármol's Japanese-Menorcan dining room.
- **[Torralbenc](/en/place/restaurante-torralbenc-en)** (Alaior), the estate with its own winery on the road to Cala en Porter.
- **[Ca na Pilar](/en/place/restaurante-ca-na-pilar-en)** (Es Migjorn Gran), Víctor Lidón's two-hundred-year-old house.
- **[Es Cranc](/en/place/restaurante-es-cranc-en)** (Fornells), the traditional temple of lobster stew.
- **[El Rais](/en/place/restaurante-el-rais-en)** (Maó), the harbour's rice dishes, from the Ses Forquilles group.

To that list add a quieter, very telling recognition: in November 2024, Michelin granted **one Key** —its hotel distinction— to four properties on the island: **Torralbenc** (Alaior), **Can Albertí 1740** (Maó), **Cristine Bedfor** (Maó) and **Faustino Gran Relais & Châteaux** (Ciutadella). Two of them have their restaurant on this map. The message between the lines: what Michelin rewards in Menorca today is small-scale hospitality, the farm estate and the manor house — not the grand production.

## And the Repsol Guide? The chain of Sols

If the red guide looks at Menorca from a certain distance, the **Repsol Guide** reads it more closely every year. The timeline of its Sols draws the island's rise better than any speech:

- **The veterans:** [Smoix](/en/place/restaurante-smoix-en) (Ciutadella) and [Cafè Balear](/en/place/restaurante-cafe-balear-en) (Ciutadella), the latter with half a century of seafood and its own boat in the harbour.
- **2024 edition:** [El Romero](/en/place/restaurante-el-romero-en) (Maó) and [Sa Llagosta](/en/place/restaurant-sa-llagosta-en) (Fornells) come in.
- **2025 edition:** [Sa Pedrera d'es Pujol](/en/place/restaurante-sa-pedrera-des-pujol-en) (Sant Lluís), the island's only one that year.
- **2026 edition:** [Aquiara](/en/place/restaurante-aquiara-en) (Ciutadella), Menorca's only new Sol at the gala held in Tarragona on 16 February 2026.

Six houses have earned their Sol, and no two are alike: an urban bistro with a boutique hotel, a harbour seafood house with its own boat, a gluten-free sustainable table, the lobster stew of Fornells, an auteur country house and a tasting menu on a seventeenth-century estate. That variety is the true map of Menorcan taste.

## The story of 2026 is called Aquiara

If one symbol of the moment had to be chosen, it would be the newest Sol. **[Aquiara](/en/place/restaurante-aquiara-en)** is the restaurant of the Morvedra Nou rural hotel —a 1650 estate surrounded by olive and wild olive trees, on the road to Macarella— and it bears the signature of **José María Borrás, a chef from Maó born in 1999** who returned to the island after training at the Hofmann school and in Japan, with time spent in starred kitchens.

His proposal condenses everything this map tells: a seasonal tasting menu that translates Menorcan tradition through contemporary technique, in a dining room reached along a rural lane. As a dated reference —prices change—, in summer 2026 the house listed a **tasting menu at €130, and €210 with wine pairing**. That the island's new Sol sits seven kilometres down the road to an iconic cove, rather than on a fashionable seafront, says a great deal about how Menorca understands luxury.

## How to use this map: bookings, seasons and produce

- **Always book — weeks ahead in July and August.** These are dining rooms with few tables; spontaneity here is paid for in disappointment.
- **Expect seasonality.** A good number of these houses —especially those on estates and in hotels— open from spring to autumn and adjust days and hours each season: confirm on the official website or by phone before going.
- **The shoulder months are the secret.** May, June, September and October offer the same level with half the pressure: the best time for the big tables on this map.
- **Follow the produce, not the trophy.** The thread running through the eleven recommendations and every Sol is the same: red prawns, lobster, Mahón-Menorca DOP cheese, market gardens and the island's wine. We devote [a whole article](/en/article/ciutadella-red-prawn) to the Ciutadella red prawn —the most singular product in this pantry— and cover the lobster stew in [our Fornells guide](/en/article/fornells-lobster-stew).

## Our view

We have long argued that in Menorca [fine dining doesn't shout](/en/article/menorca-fine-dining), and the guides —each at its own pace— keep reaching the same conclusion. The missing star is a statistical anecdote; the reality is an island where, in three years, Sols, Keys and recommendations have flowered around a single idea: local produce treated with respect, in dining rooms where someone still explains which boat your fish came in on.

Our suggestion is to use the distinctions as a compass, not a destination. Book a table from this map, go unhurried, order the island's wine and let the season decide the menu. When the star arrives —and it will— it will find the hard part already done.

> Menorca has no Michelin star and has, perhaps for that very reason, the most honest table in the Balearics: here the guides chase the island, not the other way round.
